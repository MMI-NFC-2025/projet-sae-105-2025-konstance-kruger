WOMEN IN
LITERATURE

Few novels have celebrated researchers, inventors, those who observed the world through a microscope. And when they appeared, it was often as assistants, or wives of. Yet, reality was quite different: they existed, they created, they discovered but their story was simply not told.

Today, literature is beginning to repair this silence. Female authors bring back to life these forgotten figures, reinvent science heroines, and remind us that curiosity, reason and passion have never belonged to a single gender.

Writing about these women is therefore more than a literary gesture: it is a justice done to memory, a way to finally say their names those that had been erased from textbooks as from libraries.

The book tells the true story of three African-American women: Katherine Johnson, Dorothy Vaughan and Mary Jackson, who worked for NASA in the 1950s-60s.

At that time, the United States was still living under racial segregation, and these women had to fight twice as hard as women, then as Black women.

They were "human calculators": their job was to perform complex calculations by hand, so that rockets and astronauts could take off and especially return alive.

Despite their major contributions to American space successes, their names were erased from history for decades.
